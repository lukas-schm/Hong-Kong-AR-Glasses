\begin{abstract}
\small
Observational causal-effect estimates from electronic health records routinely overstate evidence: identification assumptions are not testable, diagnostics are scattered across packages, and modelling choices accumulate silently. We describe an end-to-end pipeline that converts EHR data into clinician-facing causal treatment-effect displays. Its central mechanism is a \emph{refusal-to-render contract}: every effect estimate is paired, by construction, with the diagnostic that adjudicates it; any contrast whose required probe falls below a locked fail threshold is suppressed; marginal verdicts render with the probe value surfaced alongside the estimate. The pipeline is parametric in the database adapter, the target-trial protocol, and the estimator (eight benchmarked). The reference implementation addresses ICU antibiotic stewardship and is reported here as a TRIPOD+AI-compliant retrospective in-silico evaluation, prepared for the silent-mode phase under DECIDE-AI. In a three-arm target trial of broad-spectrum antibiotic continuation in 9{,}314 sepsis-3 ICU stays, two of three pairwise contrasts are suppressed on fail verdicts (continue-vs-de-escalate: overlap fail; de-escalate-vs-cease: specification-stability fail). The third contrast, cease-vs-continue 28-day mortality, renders with three marginal verdicts attached: post-weighting balance ($33\,\%$ with $|$SMD$|<0.1$), E-value ($1.55$), and simulation-based AIPW coverage ($84\,\%$ of nominal). Eight estimators disagree by $\sim$10\,pp on the rendered contrast (matched ATT $+1.5$\,pp, doubly-robust cluster at $-3.6$ to $-4.3$\,pp, stabilised IPTW $-8.5$\,pp); this is the literature-predicted signature of the marginal balance verdict. The doubly-robust cluster around $-4$\,pp is the pipeline's worked output, not a stewardship recommendation. The end-to-end pipeline, the locked specification, the verdict thresholds, and the clinician-facing reference frontend are released as a single reproducible artefact.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Author summary}

A clinical decision-support system that displays causal treatment-effect estimates from EHR data is only as trustworthy as the diagnostics that adjudicate those estimates --- and in practice the diagnostics are usually run after the fact, with no structural link to what the clinician sees. We describe an end-to-end pipeline that goes from raw EHR tables to a clinician-facing render, with a refusal-to-render contract that binds every output to the diagnostic that adjudicates it: estimates whose probe falls in a locked fail band are suppressed; estimates whose probe is in a marginal band render with the verdict surfaced inline. We instantiate the pipeline as a reference implementation for ICU antibiotic stewardship, reported retrospectively here under TRIPOD+AI; silent-mode and human-in-the-loop phases under DECIDE-AI are the named next steps. On a 9{,}314-stay sepsis-3 cohort, the pipeline suppresses two of three pairwise contrasts and renders the third with three marginal verdicts attached --- a cessation effect that is fragile in three independent ways: eight benchmark estimators disagree by roughly 10 percentage points, two-thirds of confounders fail post-weighting balance, and the E-value indicates a clinically plausible unmeasured confounder could fully explain the doubly-robust cluster. The pipeline's job is to expose that fragility before any of the three arms reaches a clinician, not to argue that one arm is safer than another.

\section{Introduction --- end-to-end causal CDSS from EHR data}

Causal-inference methods for observational EHR data are now standard equipment: target-trial emulation \cite{hernan2016target}, doubly-robust estimation including double/debiased machine learning (DML) \cite{chernozhukov2018dml}, propensity overlap, E-values \cite{vanderweele2017evalue}, calibration assessment, and label-shuffle placebos. Each is well documented in isolation. In practice, however, they are seldom assembled into a single workflow, and the most common failure mode of an applied causal-ML paper is not the absence of any one diagnostic but the diffuse, unauditable way the diagnostics are bolted on after the fact. The reader sees an effect estimate; the diagnostic that would tell them whether to trust it lives in a different table, a different notebook, or no longer exists. Integrative templates exist (Doutreligne et al.\ 2024 \cite{doutreligne2024stepbystep}; the broader target-trial-emulation literature) but they describe \emph{what} an analyst should do; they do not bind the rendering of an effect estimate to the verdict of its diagnostic.

This paper does not introduce a new estimator. It describes a pipeline whose contribution is a \emph{refusal-to-render contract}: a formal binding between probes and rendered outputs such that an effect estimate cannot be displayed unless its supporting diagnostic has been computed and has not failed. We define the contract in \S\ref{sec:contract} as a small set of probe-to-render rules, and we argue --- and demonstrate, in the worked example --- that this discipline is the single cheapest improvement available to applied observational causal-ML before any new methodological work. The components of the pipeline are familiar (target-trial protocol; DML and a benchmark grid that includes DRLearner, AIPW, stabilised IPTW, overlap-weighted IPTW \cite{li2018overlap}, TMLE \cite{vanderlaan2011tmle}, $g$-computation, and propensity matching; multinomial generalized propensity score; E-values; balance and overlap diagnostics; simulation-based coverage; specification-stability checks); the contract is what binds them.

We instantiate the pipeline on a representative observational decision: continuation versus de-escalation versus cessation of broad-spectrum antibiotics at 72\,h in ICU sepsis. We choose this case because (i) it is the canonical confounding-by-indication problem --- clinician-intent variables (frailty gestalt, code status, undocumented improvement, ID-consult input) are partially or fully unobserved in any retrospective EHR study and the pipeline must therefore be able to estimate \emph{and} to publicly fail; (ii) the downstream consequences of a guard mis-firing are clinically high-stakes (antimicrobial stewardship and patient mortality), so the cost of a false-confidence estimate is concrete; and (iii) the question admits external validation on a second large database. On MIMIC-IV the pipeline suppressed two of the three pairwise contrasts and returned a fragile verdict on the third. We present the resulting numbers as the pipeline's worked output, not as a stewardship recommendation. The pipeline itself is intended to generalise to other treatment-policy questions in EHR data and to other databases; the worked example here instantiates it on MIMIC-IV and releases a parallel eICU-CRD database adapter for the per-hospital transportability check that subsequent live phases will require.

\paragraph{Related work and what is new.} Five strands of prior work are most directly comparable. (i)~Target-trial-emulation frameworks for EHRs \cite{hernan2016target,wang2026operational} specify how trial elements map onto observational data and when identification can or cannot be supported; they do not specify what to render once an estimate is produced. (ii)~Step-by-step causal-analysis templates for EHRs \cite{doutreligne2024stepbystep} prescribe what an analyst should do; they leave the rendering decision to the analyst. (iii)~Estimator-specific algorithms --- including federated target-trial emulation on MIMIC-IV and eICU-CRD across hundreds of hospitals \cite{sun2025federated} --- improve how a single estimator is computed; they do not gate whether the estimate is displayed. (iv)~Scoping reviews of ICU observational causal inference \cite{allen2023scoping} document systematic under-reporting of overlap, balance, and sensitivity diagnostics in published work; they prescribe but do not enforce. (v)~Reinforcement-learning-for-sepsis transportability studies \cite{wornow2025transportability} show that policies trained on ICU data routinely fail to transport across institutions, motivating per-site verdict surfacing for any CDSS that consumes such estimates.

We differ from all five on a single structural axis: in the pipeline presented here, every effect estimate is bound, by code, to the diagnostic that adjudicates it, and any contrast whose required probe falls below a locked fail threshold is structurally inaccessible in the released artefact (suppressed from the manuscript table, the CDSS display, and the JSON payload). On the CDSS side, our verdict-token rendering is methodologically adjacent to the clinician-informed evaluation movement in *npj Digital Medicine* (e.g.\ CLIX-M \cite{clixm2025}), the early-stage clinical-evaluation reporting guideline DECIDE-AI \cite{decideai2022}, and the long-standing CDSS-design literature on success and failure modes \cite{sutton2020cdss}; we differ in tying the rendering decision to a *causal*-validity diagnostic suite rather than to a predictive-validity or explanation-quality suite. Multicenter prospective CDSS evidence (e.g.\ the MERIDIAN staphylococcus-bacteremia stepped-wedge trial \cite{meridian2025}) is the eventual CONSORT-AI \cite{consortai2020} standard the reference implementation is built toward but does not yet meet --- the present manuscript is a TRIPOD+AI retrospective in-silico evaluation, prepared for the first live phase under DECIDE-AI.

\subsection{The refusal-to-render contract}\label{sec:contract}

We formalise the gating discipline as a contract between probes and rendered outputs. For a target estimand $\theta$ (a pairwise ATE, ATT, or ATO on a chosen outcome), the pipeline evaluates a fixed set $\mathcal{P} = \{P_1, \dots, P_k\}$ of probes, each of which returns a verdict $v_i \in \{\text{pass}, \text{marginal}, \text{fail}\}$ together with the quantitative diagnostic value that produced it (e.g.\ \% of cohort in common support, $|$SMD$|<0.1$ fraction, E-value, simulation coverage). For each rendered surface $S \in \{\text{manuscript table}, \text{CDSS individual-risk display}, \text{JSON payload}\}$ the pipeline applies a render rule
\[
  R_S(\theta) \;=\; \begin{cases}
    \text{display}(\hat{\theta}, \text{CI}, \{(P_i, v_i)\}_i) & \text{if } \forall P_i \in \mathcal{P}_S^{\text{req}}: v_i \neq \text{fail} \\
    \text{suppress with reason}(\{P_i: v_i = \text{fail}\}) & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
\]
where $\mathcal{P}_S^{\text{req}} \subseteq \mathcal{P}$ is the probe subset required for surface $S$. A \emph{pass} verdict signals an unqualified render; a \emph{marginal} verdict permits the render but commits the pipeline to surface the probe value alongside the estimate so the reader can attribute the fragility; a \emph{fail} verdict suppresses the row. The asymmetry --- marginal does not suppress, fail does --- is deliberate: a guarded pipeline should not refuse every imperfect estimate (the de-escalate arm would refuse essentially everything), but it must refuse estimates whose supporting probe value lies in the regime where the literature treats the probe as actively diagnostic of bias.

\paragraph{Verdict thresholds (locked in \texttt{preregistration.yaml}).} The thresholds are conservative defaults drawn from the relevant methodological literature; they are locked at the pipeline level rather than tuned per question.

The thresholds are deliberately conservative on the \emph{fail} side: the literature on each probe identifies the relevant cliff (Crump et al.\ 2009 \cite{crump2009dealing} and Petersen et al.\ 2012 \cite{petersen2012positivity} on positivity; Austin 2009 \cite{austin2009smd} on SMD as a balance diagnostic; VanderWeele \& Ding 2017 \cite{vanderweele2017evalue} on the E-value scale). The \emph{marginal} band is a calibrated halo around the cliff: it acknowledges that observational EHR cohorts rarely produce probe values squarely above the pass threshold and that refusing every imperfect contrast would empty the rendered output. The \emph{fail} threshold is the binding constraint of the contract.

\paragraph{Properties.} Three properties follow from this construction. (i)~\emph{Completeness}: the pipeline cannot render an effect estimate whose required probe is missing --- the rendering function depends on the probe's existence as a code-level pre-condition. (ii)~\emph{Auditability}: every render carries the verdict of every required probe (pass/marginal/fail tokens in Table~\ref{tab:summary}, full diagnostic values in the JSON payload); every suppress carries an attributable reason. (iii)~\emph{Locality}: each surface $S$ has its own $\mathcal{P}_S^{\text{req}}$, so a probe that fails identification (overlap, E-value) suppresses an effect from the manuscript table, while a probe that fails individual-level reliability (per-arm calibration, Mahalanobis OOD) suppresses an arm from the CDSS without removing the population-level estimate from the manuscript table.

For the worked example, the required probe set for the manuscript table is $\mathcal{P}^{\text{req}}_{\text{table}} = \{$overlap, post-weighting balance, E-value, specification-stability null, simulation coverage$\}$; the CDSS render rule additionally requires $\{$per-arm calibration, Mahalanobis OOD$\}$. In MIMIC-IV the contract suppresses two of the three pairwise contrasts on a fail verdict and renders the third with multiple marginal verdicts attached (\S\ref{sec:summary}, Table~\ref{tab:summary}). The continue-vs-cease contrast renders not because it is unproblematic but because none of its required probes falls below the locked fail threshold; the marginal verdicts on balance ($33.3\,\%$ in the $30$--$50$ band), E-value ($1.55$ in the $1.25$--$2.0$ band), and simulation coverage ($84\,\%$ for AIPW in the $80$--$90$ band) are surfaced on the row alongside the point estimate.

\paragraph{Immortal-time bias.} Patients who die between hours 0 and 72 cannot be in any arm; restricting the cohort to those alive in the ICU at $T_0$ removes the bias. \textbf{Selection bias.} A single patient can have multiple eligible ICU stays; we retain only the earliest qualifying stay per \texttt{subject\_id} (within-subject correlation otherwise inflates effective $n$ and biases CIs narrow).

\paragraph{Why $T_0 = 72$\,h?} The 72\,h re-evaluation point is recommended by the Surviving Sepsis Campaign \cite{evans2021surviving} on the basis of (i) typical time to first culture finalisation (we report the cohort median directly), (ii) prudent empirical-therapy duration before stewardship review, and (iii) day-3 antibiotic-day-count distribution. The choice is nonetheless evaluable: we report a $T_0$-anchor sensitivity at 48\,h, 72\,h, 96\,h, and at the data-driven anchor ``time of first culture finalisation''. The treatment-window sensitivity ($\pm 6$, $\pm 12$, $\pm 24$\,h) is reported alongside in Figure~\ref{fig:window}.

\paragraph{Time-varying confounding caveat.} A single static classification at $T_0$ collapses what is in reality a dynamic stewardship process (re-decisions at day 5, day 7, on culture finalisation, on de-escalation guidance from the ID team). The grace-period and sustained-treatment sensitivities are partial mitigations; a fully sequential $g$-methods / Q-learning extension is identified as future work and is not addressed here. The pipeline reports the static contrast as a treatment-state effect at $T_0$, not as a policy effect.

\paragraph{Competing-risk sensitivity (future work).} A Fine--Gray subdistribution-hazard sensitivity treating ICU discharge alive as a competing event is the standard addition for the concern that early-discharged patients who are alive at hospital discharge but die afterward are differentially observable across arms. It is named in the pre-specification and identified as future work; it is not reported in the present manuscript.

\subsection{Step 2 — Identification}
The identification step lists the variables a causal estimator must condition on to recover an ATE from the joint distribution. We use the DAG encoded in \texttt{causal\_graph.yaml}, organised into six clinically interpretable groups:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item \emph{Illness severity}: SOFA, SAPS-II, lactate, vasopressor use, MAP.
\item \emph{Infection markers}: CRP, WBC, temperature, culture results (positive/negative/pending), infection-source flags.
\item \emph{Organ dysfunction}: AKI stage (KDIGO), PaO$_2$/FiO$_2$ ratio, ventilation, creatinine, bilirubin.
\item \emph{Treatment history}: days on antibiotics at $T_0$, prior antibiotic classes (carbapenem, glycopeptide, broad $\beta$-lactam, aminoglycoside).
\item \emph{Trajectory}: $\Delta$ SOFA, WBC, lactate, temperature, creatinine from $t=0$ to $t=72$\,h.
\item \emph{Demographics}: age, sex, immunosuppression, Charlson CCI, emergency admission, insurance.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Informative missingness (F5) and collider caveat.} ``Procalcitonin not measured'' is itself a clinical signal --- it co-varies with suspicion-of-bacterial-infection and therefore with treatment assignment. For every numeric confounder we add a binary \texttt{\_\_missing} indicator alongside the imputed value; downstream models receive both. 18 indicators are produced. Missingness indicators are not universally bias-reducing: when testing behaviour is downstream of latent severity \emph{and} treatment intent, conditioning on a missingness indicator can open a collider path \cite{groenwold2020missingness}. We therefore report a sensitivity analysis in which the same ATE is re-estimated \emph{without} missingness indicators included as predictors; the indicators are also explicitly excluded from the de-escalate-arm calibration probe to avoid the same hazard.

\paragraph{Clinical-intent proxies (WS4).} The 72-hour decision is partly driven by clinician intent variables that the original adjustment set treated as fully unobserved. We extract structured proxies for four of them from MIMIC-IV: code status at $T_0$ (chartevents itemid 223758), palliative-care consult before $T_0$ (POE \texttt{Consults}, subtype \texttt{Palliative Care}), infectious-disease consult before $T_0$ (POE \texttt{Consults}, subtype \texttt{Infectious Disease}), and source-control procedure before $T_0$ (procedureevents itemid list documented in \texttt{variables/clinical\_intent.py}). Patients in comfort-measures-only status at $T_0$ are \emph{excluded} from the analytic cohort rather than adjusted for: they are not eligible for the treatment policy of interest, and conditioning on CMO conflates goals-of-care transition with bacterial-disease reasoning. The four proxies are added to the adjustment set; an ablation removing them (\texttt{Without clinical intent} feature set) quantifies their marginal contribution. The residual unobserved layer --- bedside-gestalt frailty, undocumented improvement, full goals-of-care reasoning, microbiology certainty --- is enumerated in the unmeasured-confounders register and feeds the E-value interpretation.

\paragraph{F13 --- Administrations, not orders.} Arm classification uses ICU \texttt{inputevents} (the actual IV-pump administrations) rather than \texttt{prescriptions} (orders). A patient with a 60-hour ``continue'' order that nursing held at 70\,h is correctly coded as \emph{cease} on administrations but \emph{continue} on orders. For stays with no IV record (e.g., antibiotics given on the ward) we fall back to \texttt{prescriptions}; about one third of stays fall back this way. Treatment-arm prevalence in the final cohort (post-WS4) is 79.1\,\% continue, 4.6\,\% de-escalate, 16.3\,\% cease.

\paragraph{Treatment taxonomy --- operational definitions.} Spectrum classifications and arm-derivation rules are formalised in \texttt{definitions/} \texttt{treatment\_taxonomy.yaml}, which is the single source of truth and is versioned with the manuscript. Specifically: (i) IV vancomycin is classified as broad-spectrum (anti-MRSA continuation); \emph{oral} vancomycin and \emph{oral} metronidazole are excluded from arm classification and coded as the \emph{C.\,difficile} therapy outcome. (ii) Antifungals (fluconazole, micafungin, caspofungin, voriconazole, amphotericin) and antivirals (acyclovir, oseltamivir, ganciclovir, valacyclovir) are flagged but excluded from arm classification; concurrent presence is recorded as a deviation flag for subgroup analysis. (iii) Mixed regimens (any broad plus any narrow) are classified as \emph{continue} (the broadest active agent wins); a \texttt{dual\_gram\_negative} flag is recorded separately. (iv) Same-agent route changes (e.g., IV→PO cefepime) are flagged \texttt{route\_change\_only} but do not change the arm; a sensitivity that re-classifies them as \emph{de-escalate} is reported. (v) Short-course peri-operative prophylaxis ($\leq 24$\,h narrow-spectrum, no continuation) is excluded from arm classification (\texttt{prophylactic\_only} flag). The complete agent-by-agent assignment table is included in the supplementary appendix and as \texttt{treatment\_taxonomy.yaml} in the released code.

\subsection{Step 3 — Estimation}
We estimate the ATE in two complementary ways:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Population ATE} via a doubly-robust Double Machine Learning (DML) wrapper from \textsc{EconML}~\cite{econml}, with cross-fitting (5 folds). The propensity model is a random forest; the outcome model is a probability-returning logistic regression wrapped in a small adapter so DML's residualisation works on a Bernoulli outcome. The final stage is a Ridge regression. The same wrapper supports LinearDML, DRLearner and a CausalForestDML variant (honest splits, cross-fit nuisances).
  \item \textbf{Patient-level absolute risks} via a T-Learner: one logistic mortality model per arm, all sharing the same feature matrix. Per-patient risk contributions are decomposed in logit space from the \emph{same} logistic that produced the displayed risk, ensuring the displayed contributions sum (in log-odds) to the displayed risk delta.
\end{enumerate}
Continuous outcomes (VFD-28, VaPFD-28, ICU LOS) are clipped to clinical bounds inside the predictor, and the propensity score is clipped at 0.05 / 0.95 — the standard observational-pharma-epi convention — instead of the EconML default 0.001, which lets near-zero-propensity rows dominate weighting.

The imputer (median for numerics) lives \emph{inside} each cross-fit pipeline, not on the full training matrix, so test-fold medians do not leak into training. An outer pre-impute remains in place only to satisfy EconML's finite-input validation.

\subsection{Step 4 — Vibration analysis}
We sweep three axes in addition to the bootstrap:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item \emph{Estimator}: DML, LinearDML, DRLearner, T-Learner, CausalForestDML.
\item \emph{Feature set}: all confounders, no-infection-markers, no-trajectory, no-treatment-history, severity+demographics.
\item \emph{Treatment-classification window}: $\pm 6$\,h, $\pm 12$\,h, $\pm 24$\,h around $T_0$.
\end{enumerate}
The vibration grid (Supplementary Figure~S1) is one robustness probe; a robust effect should be a tight column of CIs across the grid, an unstable effect a smear. The post-WS4 multinomial-GPS benchmark (\S\ref{sec:estimator_benchmarking}, Table~\ref{tab:benchmark}) is the primary robustness check the manuscript relies on.

\subsection{Step 5 --- Diagnostic-gated probes and deployment guardrails}
We separate \emph{identification-layer} probes (which interrogate the conditions under which a causal estimate is admissible) from \emph{deployment-layer} probes (which interrogate the reliability of an individual-level risk display). Each is paired with a downstream gate: a failing identification probe suppresses the corresponding effect from the rendered results; a failing deployment probe suppresses the corresponding arm in the CDSS prototype.

\paragraph{Identification-layer probes.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item \emph{Positivity / overlap (F7)} --- multi-diagnostic panel: per-arm propensity histogram, standardised mean differences before and after weighting, effective sample size (ESS), tail-weight share, density overlap in covariate space. The 0.05 / 0.95 trimming threshold is reported as a sensitivity (0.01, 0.05, 0.10) and an overlap-weighted estimand \cite{li2018overlap} is also reported as the clipping-free alternative.
\item \emph{Post-weighting balance (WS6)} --- the share of confounders with $|$SMD$|<0.1$ \cite{austin2009smd} after IPTW weighting. On the headline contrast (continue vs.\ cease, post-WS4 cohort) this share is $33.3\,\%$: two thirds of the 38 confounders remain imbalanced after weighting, and the maximum stabilised weight is $3.11$, with the top 5\,\% of weights carrying 8.2\,\% of the total mass. Under the locked verdict thresholds (Table~\ref{tab:verdict_thresholds}) this value sits in the \emph{marginal} band ($30$--$50\,\%$), not the \emph{fail} band ($<30\,\%$); the render rule therefore admits the contrast but commits the pipeline to surface the value alongside every rendered estimate. The overlap histogram passes ($82.8\,\%$ in common support, $\geq 70$ pass threshold); the marginal balance verdict is the more diagnostic of the two for this contrast and is highlighted explicitly in Table~\ref{tab:summary}.
\item \emph{E-value (F10)} --- minimum confounder strength, on the risk-ratio scale, needed to explain the ATE away \cite{vanderweele2017evalue}. Reported for both the point estimate and the CI bound nearest the null.
\item \emph{Specification-stability null (F11)} --- distribution of ATEs after shuffling treatment labels within the cohort. We \emph{do not} interpret this probe as a test of exchangeability or of omitted-variable bias; it tests whether the estimator can distinguish structured association in the data from label noise under the chosen specification (\S Methods). With only 5 shuffles in the present run, the empirical q95 is reported as an order-of-magnitude check and not as a calibrated hypothesis test; a higher-replicate version is identified as future work.
\item \emph{Simulation-based coverage (F21)} --- a parametric simulation calibrated on cohort marginals, with a known ground-truth ATE, on which the empirical coverage of the nominal-95\,\% CI of each estimator is read off (Table~\ref{tab:coverage}). Replaces an earlier ``empirical CI coverage'' metric that was uninterpretable without a known truth. The simulation result is itself a probe outcome --- the estimator-dependence of coverage feeds the disagreement narrative in \S\ref{sec:estimator_benchmarking}.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Deployment-layer probes.} These do not bear on identification: a perfectly calibrated prognostic model can still be biased under unmeasured confounding. They gate \emph{individual-level} displays in the prototype CDSS.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item \emph{Per-arm calibration (F12)} --- Brier score, intercept, slope of logit($\hat p$) versus observed. Gates whether an arm's per-patient risk is shown.
\item \emph{Mahalanobis out-of-distribution check} --- flags the input record as outside the 99\textsuperscript{th} percentile of the training cohort.
\end{enumerate}
Within the architecture of this paper the CDSS plays a specific role: it is the second rendered surface --- distinct from the manuscript table --- on which the refusal-to-render contract is exercised. Reading it as a unit test for the contract is the correct frame: if $R_{\text{CDSS}}$ permits an effect estimate to appear without the deployment-layer probes (per-arm calibration, Mahalanobis OOD) having been evaluated and passed, the contract is broken. The prototype is not a candidate clinical tool, has not undergone prospective evaluation, and is not validated for stewardship use; we describe its rendered surface below because the second-surface render rule is part of the contribution.

\paragraph{What the clinician sees.} For an admitted ICU patient on broad-spectrum antibiotics approaching the 72\,h re-evaluation point, the CDSS surface displays four elements arranged top-to-bottom:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item \emph{Patient context strip.} Admission demographics, current SOFA / SAPS-II, current vasopressor and ventilation status, days on antibiotics, current culture status (positive / negative / pending), and the editable confounder inputs the clinician can override.
\item \emph{Per-arm absolute-risk panel.} For each arm \emph{the contract permits to render}, a per-patient 28-day mortality risk estimate, a CI, and the marginal verdict tokens for the contrast the arm participates in. On MIMIC-IV the panel currently shows two arms (continue, cease); the de-escalate arm is structurally absent --- replaced by a single line of text naming the failed probe (per-arm calibration slope $0.34$) and the rule the contract applied.
\item \emph{Banner row.} Three optional banners light up based on the deployment-layer probes: an overlap-warning banner if the comparison being viewed has overlap below the marginal threshold, a Mahalanobis OOD banner if the patient's covariate vector exceeds the 99\textsuperscript{th}-percentile threshold, and a fragility banner if any required probe on the rendered contrast is in the marginal band (the rendered continue-vs-cease contrast on MIMIC-IV triggers this banner on three marginal verdicts: balance, E-value, and AIPW coverage).
\item \emph{Per-patient explanation panel.} A logit-space decomposition of the displayed risk into its top-five-by-magnitude contributions. The decomposition uses the same logistic that produced the displayed risk, so contributions sum (in log-odds) to the displayed risk-delta --- the visible explanation is, by construction, the model's actual computation rather than a post-hoc approximation. This addresses the \cite{clixm2025} concern that XAI displays often correlate poorly with the underlying causal computation.
\end{itemize}

The render rule $R_{\text{CDSS}}$ guarantees three structural properties of the surface. First, the clinician cannot see a per-patient risk for an arm whose required deployment-layer probe has failed; the failed probe is named in the suppression line. Second, every rendered estimate carries its marginal verdicts inline; the clinician does not have to navigate elsewhere to learn that the displayed number is fragile. Third, the JSON payload that produced the rendered surface is persisted with every patient interaction and is the unit of analysis for the silent-mode evaluation that is the named next phase of clinical evaluation under DECIDE-AI \cite{decideai2022}.

We retain calibration here because the prototype CDSS shows per-patient absolute risks, and a poorly calibrated arm makes that display dishonest regardless of whether the underlying causal estimate is well-identified. We fit a 5-fold cross-validated logistic mortality model within each arm and computed Brier score, intercept, and slope (Figure~\ref{fig:calibration}). \subsection{Specification-stability null (label-shuffle)}
A bootstrap quantifies the sampling distribution of the estimator under the assumed specification; it cannot tell us whether the specification is right, and it does not test exchangeability, omitted-variable bias, or any other identification assumption. As a complementary \emph{specification-stability} check we shuffle treatment labels within the cohort, re-fit DML, and read off the distribution of ATEs the model produces under label noise. An observed effect that falls inside this null distribution indicates only that the estimator is unable to distinguish the structured association in the data from random label assignment under the chosen specification --- this is informative but should not be interpreted as evidence of causal validity in the opposite direction. Figure~\ref{fig:permutation} shows the result for 5 shuffles per pair. \subsection{Multi-horizon mortality trajectory (WS11)}
\label{sec:trajectory}

The mortality outcome is reported at four endpoints rather than one. We derive \texttt{mortality\_7d}, \texttt{mortality\_14d}, \texttt{mortality\_21d}, and \texttt{mortality\_28d} from \texttt{(dod $-$ decision\_time)} on the same cohort denominator (the F16 follow-up censoring NaN's all four horizons together so the four endpoints are like-for-like comparable). The four horizons are framed as four discrete marginal-risk-difference contrasts of \emph{observed treatment state at $T_0$}; the trajectory shape they trace is a visualisation of those four discrete contrasts, not a continuous-time hazard model. Per-patient absolute risks under each arm at each horizon are predicted by a T-Learner trained per (arm, horizon), and a \emph{joint} bootstrap (one cohort resample applied to all four horizons simultaneously) supplies a coherent CI band around the trajectory. The de-escalate arm is suppressed in the rendered output at every horizon by the deployment-layer calibration probe (extended to (arm $\times$ horizon) granularity in WS11). The chart connector is piecewise-linear with per-horizon CI bars by default; a cubic interpolation is available as an explicit smoothing layer.

\paragraph{Why discrete-time T-Learners and not a Cox / RSF / discrete-hazard survival model?} A proper survival model with cross-fitted nuisances would be a more parsimonious description of the same underlying process. We use the per-horizon T-Learner because it produces directly-displayable per-arm absolute risks for the CDSS prototype — a Cox hazard ratio does not. The trajectory shape produced by the four discrete T-Learners agrees with the AIPW-derived ATE trajectory (\S\ref{sec:estimator_benchmarking}) within roughly 0.5\,pp at every horizon, and the pipeline's diagnostics (overlap, calibration, E-value) apply at each horizon separately. Reviewers preferring a survival-model framing will find the equivalence in the supplementary appendix; the substantive conclusions are unchanged.

\paragraph{What the trajectory shows.} For the continue-vs-cease contrast, the doubly-robust ATE is \emph{near zero at day 7} ($+0.76$\,pp [$-0.50$, $+2.24$] under AIPW) and \emph{progressively widens} to $\approx -4$\,pp by day 28 ($-4.02$\,pp [$-5.27$, $-2.59$]). The pure-weighting estimators (IPTW, overlap-weighted ATO) show the same monotone widening but at larger magnitudes; the propensity-matched ATT, by contrast, remains a slight \emph{positive} $1$--$3$\,pp at every horizon, indicating the marginal effect at every horizon is driven by the non-overlap region rather than the matched subpopulation (\S\ref{sec:estimator_benchmarking}). The reader can therefore see, in the same figure, both \emph{when} the contrast emerges in time and \emph{which estimators} agree on its magnitude.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The cross-estimator spread is itself a robustness verdict. Doubly-robust estimators agree on $\approx -4$\,pp on the global covariate support; the matched ATT, restricted to the subpopulation that satisfies overlap, is essentially zero. \section{Operationalisation and deployment readiness}\label{sec:operationalisation}

The previous section reports the worked example's empirical output. This section reports the pipeline's properties \emph{as a software artefact}: its parametricity across databases, the locked statistical specification, the staging of the manuscript against the standard clinical-AI reporting guidelines, and the engineering choices that make the pipeline re-runnable by an independent team.

\subsection{Pipeline parametricity --- the eICU-CRD adapter}
\label{sec:eicu}

The pipeline is parametric in the database adapter: a second framing module (\texttt{framing/} \texttt{eicu\_framing.py}) maps the eICU-CRD schema \cite{pollard2018eicu} to the same target-trial protocol (sepsis-3 cohort, first broad-spectrum order from the eICU \texttt{medication} table, $T_0 = 72$\,h, $\pm 12$\,h treatment-window classification, the same 34+4 confounder set with documented eICU-specific measurement caveats). eICU spans 200+ US hospitals, which is what makes it the appropriate adapter for a per-hospital subgroup analysis that directly addresses the institution-specific stewardship-pattern concern (formulary, ID-consult culture, stop-order policies) raised by the transportability literature \cite{wornow2025transportability}. We report the adapter and its measurement-mapping decisions here; the end-to-end run on eICU is in progress at the time of submission and the results table will be added in the final version. The point of including the adapter in this manuscript is to document the pipeline's parametricity --- that the same artefact runs on two distinct EHR schemas under the same locked specification --- rather than to claim a completed external-validation result on eICU.

\subsection{Statistical pre-specification}
\label{sec:prespec}

A locked pre-specification document accompanies the source code (released alongside this manuscript as \texttt{paper/preregistration.yaml}, archived to Zenodo with a citable DOI). Primary analysis: continue-vs-cease, 28-day mortality, DML with random-forest propensity and logistic-regression outcome on the full confounder set under the $\pm 12$\,h treatment window. All other estimators, feature sets, windows, outcomes, and contrasts are secondary or sensitivity, with multiple-testing adjustment (Benjamini--Hochberg on the family of secondary outcomes; Bonferroni on the family of pairwise treatment contrasts). The pre-specification is acknowledged as retrospective for this manuscript; future runs of the pipeline (on additional databases or for related stewardship questions) will use the same locked spec.

Confidence-interval construction is estimator-specific and is reported per estimator (\S Methods): EconML's asymptotic CIs for DML/DRLearner; bootstrap-percentile (500 replicates; 200 for the headline benchmark) for AIPW, IPTW, TMLE, $g$-computation, and the T-learner; cluster handling is not required after one-stay-per-subject deduplication. Coverage of the nominal-95\,\% CI is read off a parametric simulation calibrated on the cohort marginals (true ATE $\tau = -4$\,pp, $N=2000$, 100 sims, 150 bootstrap reps per fit; \texttt{experiments/simulation\_coverage.py}). Coverage is highly estimator-dependent and is itself a probe of the manuscript's claims (Table~\ref{tab:coverage}).

\subsection{Deployment readiness --- reporting-guideline staging}\label{sec:deployment_readiness}

The reference implementation is a \textbf{retrospective in-silico evaluation} on MIMIC-IV --- end-to-end pipeline execution on a historical cohort with no live clinician interaction. The pipeline runs end-to-end, produces every required identification-layer and deployment-layer probe, and applies the locked render rules of \S\ref{sec:contract}; the clinician-facing surface is exercised on simulated user requests rather than on a live ICU. This places the current manuscript under the \textbf{TRIPOD+AI} \cite{tripodai2024} reporting guideline (clinical-prediction / clinical-inference models) and \emph{prepares} the pipeline for the first phase covered by \textbf{DECIDE-AI} \cite{decideai2022} (early-stage clinical evaluation beginning at silent-mode integration). Multicenter effectiveness evaluation on the \textbf{CONSORT-AI} \cite{consortai2020} pattern is the eventual long-term goal. We summarise the evidence each subsequent phase would require in Table~\ref{tab:decide_ai} so that the deployment-evidence gap is structurally legible rather than implicit. DECIDE-AI does not itself define numbered stages 1--4; the phases below are the reporting standards applicable at each step of the evaluation pipeline, not a numbered scheme internal to any single guideline.

\paragraph{Regulatory and stewardship context.} A stewardship-focused CDSS that displays per-patient treatment-effect estimates would, in the United States, plausibly fall under the FDA's Software-as-a-Medical-Device (SaMD) framework as a Class II decision-support tool (intent: inform a serious-condition treatment decision; clinician retains the override); the FDA's Total Product Lifecycle (TPLC) approach would govern the post-market evaluation. In the European Union the same artefact is in scope of the Medical Device Regulation (MDR) and the Artificial Intelligence Act high-risk category for healthcare AI; Article 12 traceability requires the kind of structured render-decision logging the present pipeline already produces (\texttt{diagnostics\_summary.json} payload, suppression-with-reason rows). At the clinical-guideline level, the 72\,h re-evaluation point used as $T_0$ in the worked example is the antimicrobial-stewardship recommendation of the Surviving Sepsis Campaign \cite{evans2021surviving}; the verdict-token surface is intended to support stewardship review rather than to replace it. We do not present any of these regulatory mappings as compliance claims --- the pipeline is at the retrospective in-silico phase, and the regulatory clock for live deployment has not started --- but we name them here because the structural properties of the pipeline (auditable render decisions, locked verdict thresholds, versioned configuration artefacts) are the properties that any subsequent regulatory submission would require.

\subsection{Engineering choices that support deployment}\label{sec:engineering}

A pipeline that is going to gate a CDSS render rule must satisfy engineering properties that do not appear in a typical methods paper. We list the choices explicitly here so the reader can audit them.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item \emph{Pluggable database adapter.} The framing modules in \texttt{framing/} implement the same target-trial protocol against two distinct EHR schemas (MIMIC-IV, eICU-CRD). The pipeline is parametric in the adapter.
\item \emph{Versioned configuration artefacts.} \texttt{preregistration.yaml} locks the analysis plan; \texttt{definitions/} \texttt{causal\_graph.yaml} locks the adjustment set and unmeasured-confounder register; \texttt{definitions/} \texttt{treatment\_taxonomy.yaml} locks the agent-by-agent arm-classification rules. The verdict thresholds (Table~\ref{tab:verdict_thresholds}) live in \texttt{preregistration.yaml} and are released alongside the manuscript.
\item \emph{Deterministic and resumable execution.} All bootstrap and simulation runs accept a seed; long-running jobs (sensitivity grid, simulation coverage) write per-fit parquet logs and are resumable from the last completed fit (\texttt{run\_sensitivity\_} \texttt{respawn.sh}). This is what makes a multi-day pipeline operationally tractable.
\item \emph{In-pipeline imputation.} Median imputation lives inside each cross-fit pipeline rather than once outside it, preventing test-fold medians from leaking into training and preserving the orthogonality argument that makes DML robust to nuisance-estimation error.
\item \emph{Structured JSON payload.} Every render is accompanied by a \texttt{diagnostics\_summary.json} that lists every required probe, its quantitative value, and its verdict; suppressions are emitted as structured rows with the failing probe and its diagnostic value. The JSON schema is the persistent interface the CDSS surface consumes.
\item \emph{Reporting checklists.} TRIPOD+AI \cite{tripodai2024} is provided as supplementary material and is the standard the current manuscript reports against; the DECIDE-AI \cite{decideai2022} checklist is provided as a forward-looking template for the first live phase. The pipeline's outputs (the \texttt{diagnostics\_summary.json} payload, the rendered manuscript table, and the verdict-token CDSS surface) are designed so that the data items required by each checklist are already structurally available.
\end{itemize}

These choices are what allow the pipeline to be re-run by an independent team on a new database with a new estimator under the same locked specification --- the substantive precondition of any further DECIDE-AI stage.

\paragraph{Continue vs.\ Cease (rendered, three marginal verdicts attached).} The doubly-robust AIPW point estimate is $-4.02$\,pp on 28-day mortality with CI $[-5.27, -2.59]$. The two pass verdicts ($\checkmark$) are propensity overlap ($82.8\,\%$) and the specification-stability null (obs.\ $|$ATE$|$ $= 3.80$ exceeds shuffled q$95 = 1.81$). No probe falls below its fail threshold, so the render rule $R_{\text{table}}$ admits the row; the marginal verdicts are surfaced alongside the point estimate so the reader can attribute the fragility. (i)~The eight-estimator benchmark spans $\sim 10$\,pp on the same cohort under the same GPS (IPTW $-8.45$, overlap-weighted $-6.39$, $g$-comp $-4.27$, AIPW $-4.02$, TMLE $-3.63$, DML $-4.46$, matched ATT $+1.45$; \S\ref{sec:estimator_benchmarking}, Table~\ref{tab:benchmark}). (ii)~Two thirds of confounders are not balanced after weighting --- the marginal balance verdict is the more diagnostic of the identification-layer probes for this contrast. (iv)~The matched ATT, restricted to the common-support region, is essentially zero. We do not present the rendered $-4$\,pp as a stewardship recommendation. Independent randomised evidence remains the appropriate confirmatory test.

\paragraph{Continue vs.\ De-escalate (suppressed: overlap fail).} The overlap probe returns $36.2\,\%$ in common support --- below the $<50\,\%$ fail threshold (Table~\ref{tab:verdict_thresholds}). The pipeline emits a structured ``suppressed --- overlap, E-value, specification-stability'' row with the failing diagnostic values; no point estimate is displayed in the released artefact.

\paragraph{De-escalate vs.\ Cease (suppressed: specification-stability fail; CDSS additionally suppressed by calibration).} On the manuscript-table render rule the suppressing fail is the specification-stability null: the observed $|$ATE$|$ ($1.96$) lies well below the shuffled q$95$ ($2.91$), so the contract removes the row from the manuscript table. The other probes are mixed: overlap passes ($99.1\,\%$), balance passes ($50.0\,\%$), E-value is marginal ($1.39$), and simulation coverage is marginal. On the CDSS render rule a second probe fails independently --- the de-escalate-arm per-arm calibration slope ($0.34$, well below the $0.7$ pass and $0.5$ marginal floors; n=107 deaths in 429 patients) --- so the individual-risk display would be suppressed even if the manuscript-table verdict differed. The double suppression (one identification-layer fail, one deployment-layer fail) illustrates the contract's locality property: the two render rules can converge on the same decision through different probes.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{What the worked example shows.} On MIMIC-IV the pipeline suppressed two of the three pairwise contrasts on fail verdicts and rendered the third with three marginal verdicts (Table~\ref{tab:summary}). The headline substantive finding is the cross-estimator disagreement on the rendered contrast: under a single multinomial GPS on the same cohort, eight estimators span $\sim 10$\,pp on continue-vs-cease 28-day mortality, with the propensity-matched ATT --- the only estimand restricted to the overlap region --- essentially zero ($+1.45$\,pp, CI [$-2.3, +3.9$]) while the doubly-robust cluster sits at $-3.6$ to $-4.3$\,pp and pure-weighting estimators overshoot to $-6$ to $-8$\,pp. This is the literature-predicted signature of a non-overlap problem (Crump et al.\ 2009 \cite{crump2009dealing}; Petersen et al.\ 2012 \cite{petersen2012positivity}; Stuart 2010 \cite{stuart2010matching}): when post-weighting balance is poor, weighting and doubly-robust estimators borrow strength from non-overlap regions while matched-ATT does not. The contract treats the spread as confirmatory of the marginal balance verdict on the rendered row rather than as an independent surprise. Two further marginal verdicts amplify the fragile reading: simulation-based coverage of the rendered estimator (AIPW $77\,\%$) sits in the marginal band, and the E-value ($1.55$) sits in the marginal band --- a confounder with risk ratios of $\sim 1.3$ on both treatment and outcome (bedside-gestalt frailty, undocumented improvement, goals-of-care discussion, ID-consult recommendation) would explain the doubly-robust estimate away, and several such confounders are explicitly unobserved in any retrospective EHR study (we extract proxies for code status, palliative-care transitions, ID consultation, and source-control procedures, but the residual clinician-intent layer remains unobserved by design). The contract therefore does not, and the manuscript does not, present the rendered cessation effect as a stewardship recommendation; the rendered number arrives accompanied by the three marginal verdicts and the cross-estimator divergence that produced them. Independent randomised evidence remains the appropriate confirmatory test.

\paragraph{Contrasts the pipeline refuses to render.} Continue-vs-de-escalate suppresses on three independent fail verdicts (overlap $36.2\,\%$; E-value $1.00$ at the CI bound; specification-stability obs.\ $|$ATE$|$ well below the q$95$ of the shuffled null). De-escalate-vs-cease suppresses on the specification-stability fail at the manuscript-table render rule and on the per-arm calibration fail (slope $0.34$) at the CDSS render rule. With only 429 de-escalate patients (4.6\,\% of the cohort), the data are too sparse and too imbalanced for individual-level recommendations on de-escalation in this database. The pipeline's job here is to refuse: a more permissive workflow would have produced an estimate with a confident-looking confidence interval, and we would have shown it.

\paragraph{The pipeline is the contribution.} The components in this pipeline are not new --- target-trial emulation \cite{hernan2016target}, step-by-step causal-analysis templates for EHRs \cite{doutreligne2024stepbystep}, DML \cite{chernozhukov2018dml} and benchmark estimators (DRLearner, AIPW, IPTW with overlap weighting, TMLE \cite{vanderlaan2011tmle}, $g$-computation, propensity matching), E-values \cite{vanderweele2017evalue}, balance diagnostics \cite{austin2009smd}, label-shuffle nulls, and simulation-based coverage all exist in the literature. What is new is the \emph{refusal-to-render contract} formalised in \S\ref{sec:contract}: probes and renders are bound by code, not by analyst discipline, so an effect estimate that lacks an evaluated probe, or whose probe fails, is structurally absent from the released artefact (the manuscript table, the CDSS display, and the JSON payload). Step-by-step templates --- the closest related work --- prescribe what an analyst should do; they do not bind the rendering decision to the diagnostic outcome. The worked example here demonstrates the value of that binding: a permissive pipeline applied to the same MIMIC-IV cohort would have shown a confident continue-vs-cease point estimate of $-4$\,pp without flagging that the same multinomial GPS yields $+1.45$\,pp under matched-ATT, that two-thirds of confounders fail post-weighting balance, and that the $g$-computation CI covers nominal truth in $10\,\%$ of simulations. The contract makes those signals first-class outputs rather than buried sensitivity analyses.

\paragraph{What the pipeline taught us in this case.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item EHR prescriptions (orders) and administrations (\texttt{inputevents}) are not interchangeable, and the difference is non-trivial: switching the arm classifier to administrations reclassified 309 MIMIC-IV stays out of the cease arm and increased the de-escalate arm by 59\,\%. For stewardship-style questions the bedside team's discretion in holding doses is precisely the quantity of interest.
\item In-pipeline imputation is required for double-ML cross-fitting. Imputing once outside the cross-fit leaks test-fold medians into training and violates the orthogonality argument that makes DML robust to nuisance-estimation error. A global pre-impute remains only to satisfy EconML's finite-input validation.
\item The calibration probe is operationally cheap and surgically targeted: the de-escalate calibration slope of $0.34$ cannot be fixed by hyper-parameter search; it is a data-availability problem. Without this probe the prototype CDSS would have surfaced a confidently mis-calibrated risk for one of the three options. We re-classify calibration as a \emph{deployment-layer} probe (it does not bear on identification), but it remains the single most useful per-arm gate for an individual-level risk display.
\item The label-shuffle null is not a test of identification, and it does not adjudicate exchangeability or omitted-variable bias. It tests whether the estimator can distinguish structured association from random label assignment under the chosen specification. Two of three contrasts fail that check; reporting bootstrap CIs alone would have over-stated apparent stability.
\item \emph{No estimator dominates --- the doubly-robust ATEs and the matched ATT estimate different estimands on different subpopulations.} On the headline contrast, doubly-robust estimators (DML, AIPW, TMLE, $g$-computation) cluster around $-4$\,pp; pure-weighting estimators (IPTW, overlap-weighted ATO) report $-6$ to $-8$\,pp; and the propensity-matched ATT, restricted to the subpopulation that achieves adequate overlap, is essentially zero ($+1.45$\,pp [$-2.29, +3.89$]) --- the effect, on the matched subpopulation, does not survive. This estimator-to-estimator divergence is itself a robustness diagnostic and a substantively important caveat: the gap is large enough to argue that the headline doubly-robust number does not generalise straightforwardly across the cohort. Honest disclosure of estimator-to-estimator variation, made cheap by the multinomial-GPS shared-support construction, is therefore not optional --- it is the principal signal the pipeline returns for this contrast.
\item Post-weighting balance and simulation coverage --- not just propensity-histogram overlap --- are required to interpret an IPTW or doubly-robust point estimate. On this cohort, two-thirds of confounders fail $|$SMD$|<0.1$ after weighting on the headline contrast despite $82.8\,\%$ nominal overlap; and the simulation-coverage of nominal-95\,\% CIs ranges from $10\,\%$ ($g$-computation) to $100\,\%$ (TMLE at clinically uninformative widths). Both are pipeline outputs; both feed the rendered verdicts.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Limitations.} Distinguishing what the pipeline cannot estimate, what the pipeline does not yet support, and what the pipeline has not yet been clinically evaluated on is a substantive contribution of the manuscript --- the three layers each fail differently and demand different responses.

\textbf{Identification limits} --- properties of the question and the data that no pipeline implementation can resolve. (i)~Clinician treatment intent --- bedside frailty gestalt, undocumented improvement, full goals-of-care reasoning, microbiology-result certainty --- is not fully recoverable from structured EHR fields. The four added confounders (code status, palliative-care transitions, ID-consult flag, source-control procedures) close part but not all of the gap, and the marginal E-value verdict on the rendered contrast reflects this directly. (ii)~Patients in comfort-measures-only status at $T_0$ are excluded rather than adjusted for. (iii)~The contrast is operationalised as observed treatment states in a $\pm 12$\,h window around 72\,h (with sustained-treatment $\geq 48$\,h post-$T_0$ and 24\,h grace-period sensitivities), not as the effect of a deterministic policy. (iv)~A single static decision point at 72\,h collapses what is in reality a dynamic stewardship process; extending the pipeline to sequential dynamic regimes ($g$-methods, Q-learning, sequential ignorability) is named future work.

\textbf{Pipeline limits} --- properties of the implementation that further engineering would address. (i)~Two database adapters (MIMIC-IV, eICU-CRD); AmsterdamUMCdb, HiRID, and SICdb would each add a non-US healthcare-system adapter and are named future work. The framing module is intentionally pluggable for this purpose. (ii)~The specification-stability null is run at five shuffles in the present release; a $\geq 1000$-shuffle calibrated version is identified as a Stage-2 prerequisite. (iii)~\emph{C.\,difficile} is operationalised as initiation of oral vancomycin or oral metronidazole, not a positive toxin assay (MIMIC-IV does not code toxin assays consistently). This is the standard pharma-epi proxy \cite{bagdasarian2015diagnosis} but it captures only treated CDI. (iv)~Procalcitonin is excluded from the editable CDSS inputs: MIMIC-IV PCT coverage is too sparse to support its inclusion as a confounder.

\textbf{Deployment-evidence limits} --- clinical-evaluation phases that the present manuscript does not provide. (i)~The reference implementation has been evaluated retrospectively on MIMIC-IV only --- in-silico, no live clinician interaction, reported under TRIPOD+AI \cite{tripodai2024}. (ii)~No silent-mode integration; the pipeline has not yet been run prospectively on incoming ICU records (the first live phase covered by DECIDE-AI \cite{decideai2022}). (iii)~No clinician-usability instrumentation, no targeted user studies, no comparative cognitive-load metrics (early human-in-the-loop DECIDE-AI phase). (iv)~No prospective in-clinic deployment, no per-site governance, no patient-outcome instrumentation (later DECIDE-AI phase). (v)~No multicenter effectiveness evaluation analogous to MERIDIAN \cite{meridian2025} (CONSORT-AI \cite{consortai2020} standard). The roadmap and the standards applicable to each phase are named in Table~\ref{tab:decide_ai}; what the present manuscript does not provide is \emph{evidence} at the live phases, not the design that would generate that evidence.
